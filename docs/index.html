The state of children’s mental health services 2020/21

Introduction

This is the Office of the Children’s Commissioner’s (OCC) fifth annual report on the state of children’s mental health services in England. The report aims to assess children’s ability to access Children and Young People’s Mental Health Services (CYPMHS) provision in England in 2020/21 and how (if at all) this has changed over the past two years. Using new figures sourced from NHS Digital, NHS England and NHS Improvement, this report examines the following at both a national and Clinical Commissioning Group (CCG) level:

  • Numbers of children referred to and accessing CYPMHS
  • Numbers of children referred that were not accepted into treatment during the 2020/21 financial year, as proxied by the numbers not receiving two contacts with CYPMHS
  • Average waiting times
  • Spending on children’s mental health


Headline findings

  • Spending has increased for the fourth consecutive year. In 2020/21, £881 million was spent on children’s mental health services – an increase from £791 million last year.
  • The number and rate of children referred to CYPMHS has decreased for the first time since 2017/18. In 2020/21, 497,502 children were referred, a decrease from 539,000 last year. Referral rates have also dropped from 4.5% to 4% of the under 18 population.
  • NHS figures state that 2 in 5 children with a mental health disorder are accessing specialist mental health services (39.6%). However, these figures are based on prevalence data from 2004 (updated in 2017) rather than the more recent 2021 figures. Applying these new prevalence rates (which capture the rise in probable mental health disorders during the pandemic from 1 in 9 to 1 in 6 children) to ONS population estimates, we calculate the true access rate to be closer to a third of children (32%).
  • For those who are accepted into treatment, average waiting times have decreased substantially from 43 days (2019/20) to 32 days (2020/21).
  • However, many children are not getting the help they need. Over a third of children referred are still waiting for their second contact (roughly the same as last year) and almost a quarter had their referrals closed before treatment.
